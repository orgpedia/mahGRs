Introduction The textile sector has undergone many changes in the last few decades and now the technology sector has
The textile industry is changing rapidly. India is a global leader in textile manufacturing. Textile sector plays a very important role in GDP, industrial production, employment generation, exports and national economy. India is the world's second-largest producer and exporter of textiles. The textile sector contributes 2.3 per cent to India's GDP. It accounts for 13% of industrial production and 12% of exports. Textile industry is the largest contributor to employment generation in the country after agriculture, with an estimated 45 million people directly engaged in this sector. A large number of them are women and children.
The Central Government is implementing various schemes for the development of the textile sector across the country. These include Production Linked Incentive Scheme (PLI Scheme), A TUFS, Scheme for Development of Textile Sector (Power-Tex), Scheme for Integrated Textile Park, Scheme for Capacity Building of Samarth Textile Sector, Jute-Improved Cultivation and Advanced Rating Exercise (ICARE), Integrated Process Development Scheme (IPDS), Samagra Silk Development Scheme, National Handloom Development Programme, National Handicrafts Development Programme, Integrated Wool Development Programme (IWDP), etc.
The Technical Textiles sector, which is considered as an emerging sector, accounts for approximately 13% of the textile market and contributes an average of 0.7% to the country's GDP. The technical textiles sector is also contributing to other industries in a big way. The global growth rate of the technical textiles sector is estimated to grow at 4.7 per cent during 2022-2028. Technical textile industry in the country
There is a lot of scope for development and the Government of India is giving a lot of emphasis on technical textiles to boost the economy by implementing various schemes in this sector, for example, National Technical Textiles Mission and Production Linked Incentive (PLI) Scheme.
4. Maharashtra has a significant role to play in the remarkable growth story of India's textile sector. Textile industry plays a vital role in the economy of Maharashtra as it is the second largest employment generator after agriculture. The state of Maharashtra contributes 10.4 per cent of the total textile production and 10.2 per cent of the total employment in the country. In addition, the state produces 272 million kg of yarn, which is 12% of India's total yarn production.

5. Definition and Abbreviations
Definition:
1) Original cost: The cost of the equipment excluding taxes, fees and any other charges.
(ii) Capital Investment Subsidy (CIS): Subsidy fixed on the basis of the base price of eligible machinery at the prescribed rate for modernization/ expansion of the project in the textile sector.
3) Composite Components: A project involving at least two or more processes in the value chain namely spinning, weaving, ginning, pressing, garnishing and processing.
4) Diversification Project: If an existing textile plant starts off with an additional separate product in the textile chain in addition to the product it is producing, it will be considered as a diversification project.
Existing projects: Any project that existed before the announcement of this policy.
Eligible Machinery: Machinery eligible under the Centrally Sponsored Tough Scheme (including modifications made by the Central Government from time to time in this scheme) or machinery specified by the State Government (only new machinery will be included in the eligible machinery, old or refurbished machinery will not be accepted).
Eligible Amount: Amount of rebate/ incentive/ subsidy/ reimbursement etc. to be provided under various schemes under the policy.
8) Expansion Project: Any existing project which increases the production capacity by 25% of the fixed capital investment of the previous year and 25% of the total production capacity of the project will be considered as an expansion project. The project will create 10% more jobs than the current project. This expansion project will involve a combination of forward/ backward processes.
Fixed Capital Investment: Fixed capital investment will include the basic cost of plant and machinery. This does not include the cost of land and construction. The Detailed Project Report (DPR) of the new Textile Unit will include the cost of installation of the solar power plant and the cost of the solar plant (other than land and building construction and within the limit of 4 MW) in the eligible plant and machinery will also be considered while calculating the grant capital.
10) Integrated: Integration of components across the entire value chain of textiles and apparel.
Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises (MSME): The total investment in plant and machinery in a project as amended under the Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises Act, 2006 or as amended by the Central Government from time to time is Rs. 50 crore for the project.

7.7 Silk Industry:
The state of Maharashtra is a non-traditional silk producing state, producing both mulberry and tussar silk. It also ranks first in silk production among non-traditional states and 13th among 29 silk producing states. As the sericulture industry is based on agriculture and forest wealth, at present 12,809 farmers are engaged in sericulture through mulberry cultivation in an area of about 13,582 acres. In the state 853 farmers are engaged in tussar silk industry on 3963 hectares area.
Mulberry is cultivated in 27 districts of Maharashtra and tussar in 4 districts, so a total of 31 districts are involved in silk production. In the case of mulberry silk, all the districts of Aurangabad and Pune divisions are significantly ahead in silk production areas. Tussar silk is mainly found in four districts of eastern Vidarbha- Gondia, Gadchiroli, Bhandara and Chandrapur. Thus, Maharashtra is one of the major non-conventional silk producing states and silk is emerging as a major industry among farmers. With the right climate, land and environmental conditions in the state, there are opportunities for large-scale silk production in the future. Efforts will be made to increase the production of silk yarn and silk thread to produce more silk than the traditional silk producing states.
